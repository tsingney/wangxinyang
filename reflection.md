# Reflection Report – ACC102 Mini Assignment Track 2

---

This project set out to answer a question relevant to both finance students and individual investors: which S&P 500 sectors delivered the strongest risk-adjusted performance between 2020 and 2024, and how did their volatility and correlations evolve across that period? The intended audience is anyone who wants a data-driven sector comparison without access to professional terminals such as Bloomberg or WRDS. I chose this problem because sector analysis sits at the intersection of the analytical skills taught in this module and a genuine real-world investment question.
For the dataset, I used SPDR Sector ETFs downloaded via the yfinance Python library, covering all eleven GICS sectors of the S&P 500. I selected this data source for three reasons. First, it is freely accessible and publicly licensed, satisfying the module's requirement for lawful and reliable data. Second, the ETF price histories are comprehensive and well-maintained, requiring minimal cleaning. Third, the structure of the data — a ticker symbol mapped to a daily time series — directly mirrors the workflow taught in class, where a company identifier is used to retrieve financial records from a database. This made it a natural fit for applying the module's methods in an independent context.

The Python workflow followed the structure introduced across Weeks 4 to 6. I stored all key variables — start date, end date, risk-free rate, and ticker dictionary — as parameters at the top of the notebook, so the entire analysis can be rerun for any time period by changing only those values. This is the same principle as using f-strings to build flexible SQL queries, as taught in Week 6. I then wrapped the data download and the statistics calculation into two reusable functions, following the Week 6 lesson on turning notebook cells into functions for cleaner and more maintainable code. The pandas operations used throughout — pct_change for daily returns, cumprod for cumulative index, rolling for volatility windows, groupby for annual aggregation, and corr for the correlation matrix — were all introduced in the module's practical sessions. Six charts were produced using matplotlib and seaborn, covering cumulative returns, risk-return scatter, Sharpe ratio, rolling volatility, correlation, and annual rotation.

The main findings were clear and interpretable. Technology delivered the highest cumulative return and among the strongest risk-adjusted performance over the period, driven by post-COVID digital acceleration and the artificial intelligence investment cycle in 2023 and 2024. Energy showed the widest annual return swings of any sector, collapsing in 2020 when oil demand fell and surging in 2022 when the Russia-Ukraine conflict pushed energy prices sharply higher — a textbook example of how geopolitical events can dominate sector returns. Utilities and Consumer Staples consistently showed the lowest volatility, confirming their role as defensive holdings. The correlation heatmap revealed that most sectors move together with correlations above 0.7, which limits the diversification benefit of holding multiple S&P 500 sectors simultaneously. Energy and Utilities showed the weakest co-movement with Technology, making them more useful for portfolio construction.

Several limitations apply to this analysis. The ETF proxies carry small tracking errors and expense ratios relative to the pure GICS index, meaning the returns shown are marginally understated. The risk-free rate was held constant at 4.5 percent throughout, when in reality it ranged from near zero in 2020 and 2021 to above 5 percent in 2023 — this affects the Sharpe ratio calculations for different sub-periods. The COVID-19 crash in the first quarter of 2020 inflates the long-run volatility estimates for all sectors, which makes the full-period Sharpe ratios harder to interpret in isolation. Survivorship bias is also present, since the current ETF constituents reflect today's index composition rather than the composition at the start of 2020. Finally, the analysis treats sector performance in isolation, without linking it to macroeconomic drivers such as interest rate cycles, earnings growth, or credit spreads, which limits the depth of interpretation.

The most significant difficulty I encountered was the max drawdown calculation. My initial approach simply found the largest single-day loss, which was incorrect. The correct method requires computing the rolling cumulative maximum of the price index and then measuring how far each observation falls below that peak. Understanding this distinction — between a point-in-time loss and a sustained peak-to-trough decline — required me to think carefully about what the metric actually means for an investor who holds through a downturn. This was the most valuable technical learning from the project. Beyond the code, I also learned how to structure a data product for a non-technical audience: the chart titles, axis labels, and colour choices all required deliberate decisions about what the reader needs to understand at a glance, not just what the data contains.
